# Techniques Catalog

A comprehensive library of creative techniques for AI image generation. Each technique includes a description, invocation guidance, and an example prompt snippet. This catalog serves as a practical reference for the prompt-architect agent when composing prompts.

---

## Style Transfer & Transformation

### 1. Anime/Ghibli Conversion

**What it is:** Transforms photographic or realistic imagery into the soft, hand-painted aesthetic associated with Japanese animation studios. Edges become rounded, palettes shift toward warm earth tones and pastels, and facial features simplify into expressive but stylized forms.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "in the style of Studio Ghibli," "anime aesthetic with soft watercolor backgrounds," "hand-painted anime style with warm natural lighting," or "Japanese animation cel-shading with simplified features."

**Example snippet:** "A quiet countryside train station at golden hour, rendered in Studio Ghibli anime style with soft watercolor skies, rounded architecture, and a young traveler with simplified expressive features waiting on the platform."

### 2. Pixar 3D Rendering

**What it is:** Applies the distinctive character design language of modern 3D animated films: smooth subsurface-scattered skin, oversized expressive eyes, slightly caricatured proportions, and clean geometric forms with soft ambient occlusion.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "Pixar-style 3D character," "stylized 3D render with expressive eyes and smooth skin," "clean 3D animation style with subsurface scattering," or "modern animated film character design."

**Example snippet:** "A cheerful grandmother character in Pixar 3D style, with round rosy cheeks showing subsurface skin scattering, twinkling oversized eyes behind tiny spectacles, and a hand-knitted cardigan with visible yarn texture."

### 3. Voxel Conversion

**What it is:** Transforms subjects into blocky three-dimensional cube-based representations, evoking the aesthetic of Minecraft or retro video game worlds. Every surface becomes a stepped arrangement of uniform cubes with visible grid structure.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "voxel art style," "Minecraft-inspired blocky 3D," "cube-based voxel render," or "low-resolution 3D block construction."

**Example snippet:** "A medieval castle on a hilltop rendered entirely in voxel blocks, each cube visible and textured, with blocky trees surrounding the moat and a pixelated flag on the tallest tower."

### 4. 35mm Film Photography

**What it is:** Recreates the organic qualities of analog film: natural grain structure, subtle color shifts in shadows, halation around highlights, and the slightly imperfect focus that gives film its warmth and authenticity.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "shot on 35mm Kodak Portra 400," "analog film grain with warm color shift," "vintage film photography aesthetic," or "natural film stock imperfections with soft halation."

**Example snippet:** "A street musician playing saxophone under a neon sign, shot on 35mm Kodak Portra 400 with visible film grain, warm orange-shifted shadows, and soft halation bleeding from the bright neon letters."

### 5. Vintage/Retro Overlay

**What it is:** Applies period-specific visual characteristics from a defined era. Each decade has distinct signatures: 1950s travel posters use flat gouache colors, 1970s Polaroids show faded warm tones with rounded borders, 1980s aesthetics feature neon grids and chrome.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "1950s travel poster aesthetic," "faded 70s Polaroid snapshot," "80s retro neon and chrome," "1920s Art Deco illustration," or specify the exact decade and medium.

**Example snippet:** "A diner scene in 1970s Polaroid style with faded warm tones, slightly overexposed highlights, rounded white border, and the soft amber cast of an instant film left in sunlight."

### 6. Watercolor Wash

**What it is:** Simulates the behavior of watercolor paint on textured paper: pigment pooling at edges, transparent overlapping color layers, visible brush strokes, areas of white paper showing through, and the soft diffusion where wet colors meet.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "loose watercolor painting on cold-pressed paper," "transparent watercolor washes with visible paper texture," "wet-on-wet watercolor technique," or "soft bleeding watercolor edges."

**Example snippet:** "A cluster of wildflowers painted in loose watercolor washes, pigment pooling at petal edges, transparent purple layers overlapping with yellow centers, white paper visible through the thinnest strokes."

### 7. Hand-Drawn Illustration

**What it is:** Evokes the quality of manual draftsmanship: confident ink lines of varying weight, crosshatching for shadow, visible pen strokes, and the slight imperfections that distinguish hand-drawn work from digital perfection.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "hand-drawn pen and ink illustration," "clean line art with variable stroke weight," "editorial illustration style with crosshatching," or "ink drawing with minimal shading."

**Example snippet:** "An architectural sketch of a Parisian cafe corner, hand-drawn in black ink with confident variable-weight lines, light crosshatching for awning shadows, and loose gestural figures at the outdoor tables."

### 8. Chibi/Q-Version Transformation

**What it is:** Converts realistic subjects into the "super-deformed" proportional style where heads occupy roughly one-third of body height, eyes are enlarged, limbs are stubby, and recognizable features (hairstyle, clothing, accessories) are preserved in miniaturized form.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "chibi style with oversized head," "Q-version character with 2:1 head-to-body ratio," "cute super-deformed proportions," or "kawaii miniature version preserving key features."

**Example snippet:** "A chibi version of a chef character with an oversized round head taking up a third of the body, tiny stubby arms holding an enormous whisk, maintaining the original tall white hat and double-breasted jacket."

### 9. Double Exposure Merge

**What it is:** Composites two distinct visual layers into a single frame, mimicking the analog photography technique of exposing film twice. Typically a human silhouette filled with a landscape, cityscape, or abstract pattern, with the overlap creating poetic visual metaphors.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "double exposure effect," "silhouette filled with landscape," "dual-layer composite photograph," or "overlapping exposure merging portrait with nature."

**Example snippet:** "A double exposure portrait of a woman's profile silhouette filled with an autumn forest, golden leaves visible through her hair outline, tree trunks aligning with her neck, sky showing through the top of her head."

### 10. ASCII Art Rendering

**What it is:** Translates visual imagery into compositions built from typed characters, using density and arrangement of letters, numbers, and symbols to create recognizable forms. Darker areas use dense characters like @ and #, while lighter areas use periods and spaces.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "rendered as ASCII art," "character-based text art," "monospaced typewriter character illustration," or "typographic density art using keyboard symbols."

**Example snippet:** "A portrait of a cat rendered entirely in ASCII characters on a black terminal background, using @ symbols for dark fur, slashes for whiskers, and periods for highlighted areas on the nose."

### 11. Minimalist 3D

**What it is:** Reduces three-dimensional forms to their essential geometric shapes with smooth matte surfaces, soft diffused shadows, and a restrained warm palette of beige, cream, terracotta, and sage. The aesthetic emphasizes negative space and calm simplicity.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "minimalist 3D render with matte surfaces," "low-poly soft pastel 3D scene," "clean geometric forms with warm neutral palette," or "simple 3D objects with diffused studio lighting."

**Example snippet:** "A minimalist 3D still life of geometric kitchen objects on a rounded shelf: a matte cream teapot, a terracotta cylinder cup, and a sage-green cone vase, all with soft diffused shadows on a beige background."

### 12. Satirical Comic Style

**What it is:** Employs the bold visual language of editorial cartooning: exaggerated facial features, thick confident outlines, limited flat color palettes, and compositions that frame subjects in ways that amplify their most distinctive or absurd qualities.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "editorial cartoon style with exaggerated features," "satirical illustration with bold outlines," "caricature comic art with retro color palette," or "political cartoon aesthetic."

**Example snippet:** "A satirical editorial cartoon of a businessman with an enormously oversized smartphone for a head, bold black outlines, flat retro orange and teal palette, standing at a crosswalk while smaller normal-headed pedestrians stare."

### 13. Traditional Art Reinterpretation

**What it is:** Applies the visual conventions of specific historical art movements or traditions to contemporary subjects. Each tradition carries distinct rules: Ukiyo-e uses flat planes and wave-like outlines, Renaissance oil employs chiaroscuro and sfumato, Art Nouveau features organic flowing lines.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "in the style of Ukiyo-e woodblock printing," "Renaissance oil painting with chiaroscuro lighting," "Art Nouveau poster with flowing organic lines," or "Impressionist brushwork and broken color."

**Example snippet:** "A modern city skyline reimagined as a Japanese Ukiyo-e woodblock print, with flat color planes, stylized wave-pattern clouds, thin black outlines, and a large red sun behind the tallest tower."

### 14. Paper Craft Aesthetic

**What it is:** Simulates the look of physical paper construction: visible cut edges with white paper core showing, layered depth from stacked paper sheets, subtle fold lines, and the slight warping and shadow that real paper creates in three dimensions.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "paper craft style with visible cut edges," "layered paper collage with shadows between layers," "paper sculpture aesthetic," or "constructed from colored cardstock with visible folds."

**Example snippet:** "A tropical jungle scene built from layered cut paper, each plant a separate cardstock layer casting subtle shadows on the one behind it, visible white edges where the green paper was cut, and a paper parrot with folded wings."

### 15. Vector Poster Style

**What it is:** Employs the clean precision of vector graphics: perfectly smooth edges, flat color fills without gradients, geometric simplification of forms, limited palettes of four to six colors, and modernist compositional balance typical of mid-century travel and event posters.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "flat vector poster illustration," "modernist geometric poster with limited palette," "clean vector art with sharp edges," or "Swiss-style graphic design illustration."

**Example snippet:** "A vector poster of a lighthouse on coastal cliffs, four-color palette of navy, white, coral, and gold, geometric simplified forms, flat color fills with no gradients, bold sans-serif title at the bottom."

## Dimensional & Spatial Manipulation

### 16. Isometric Perspective

**What it is:** Renders scenes from a fixed elevated angle where all three axes maintain equal scale, eliminating vanishing points. This creates a systematic, map-like view that reveals spatial relationships while maintaining a clean graphic quality, commonly seen in strategy games and architectural diagrams.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "isometric perspective," "isometric 3D view at 30-degree angle," "axonometric projection without vanishing points," or "isometric pixel art scene."

**Example snippet:** "An isometric view of a cozy bookshop interior, all walls at equal 30-degree angles, showing bookshelves, a reading nook, a spiral staircase, and a cat on the counter, all rendered without perspective distortion."

### 17. Tilt-Shift Miniature

**What it is:** Applies selective focus to overhead or elevated photographs so that real-world scenes appear to be photographs of tiny scale models. Sharp focus exists only in a narrow horizontal band, with everything above and below falling into creamy bokeh, while color saturation is boosted slightly.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "tilt-shift miniature effect," "selective focus making real scene look like tiny model," "miniature diorama photography with shallow depth of field," or "Lensbaby tilt-shift overhead view."

**Example snippet:** "An overhead tilt-shift photograph of a busy harbor, only the center row of fishing boats in sharp focus, everything above and below dissolved into soft bokeh, saturated colors making the boats look like painted toy models."

### 18. Pop-Up Book Effect

**What it is:** Recreates the multi-layered paper engineering of children's pop-up books: flat illustrated panels that fold upward into three-dimensional scenes, with visible fold creases, paper tabs, and the characteristic stepped depth of mechanical paper construction.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "pop-up book illustration with paper layers," "3D paper pop-up scene with fold lines," "children's pop-up book page with layered depth," or "paper-engineered storybook scene."

**Example snippet:** "A fairy tale castle as a pop-up book page, the main tower folding upward from the center crease, flanking walls angled outward on paper tabs, a drawbridge folding flat, and tiny paper trees on stepped background layers."

### 19. Frame Breakout

**What it is:** Creates the illusion of a subject physically bursting through a two-dimensional boundary — a photograph, picture frame, card border, or wall — into the viewer's space. Debris, cracks, energy trails, or reaching limbs sell the dimensional violation.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "breaking out of the frame," "bursting through the photo border into 3D space," "dimensional breakout with debris," or "character reaching out of a picture frame with shattered glass."

**Example snippet:** "A roaring tiger leaping forward out of a gilded picture frame, one paw already in 3D space crushing the ornate gold border, wooden splinters flying, the background still flat inside the frame while the tiger's body transitions to fully rendered 3D with natural fur texture and realistic muscle definition."

### 20. Layered Depth Composition

**What it is:** Organizes the image into distinct foreground, midground, and background planes, each with different focus characteristics and atmospheric density. This mimics cinematic rack-focus and aerial perspective to create strong spatial depth.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "layered depth with sharp foreground and soft background," "cinematic depth of field with three distinct planes," "atmospheric perspective with foreground bokeh," or "rack-focus composition from near to far."

**Example snippet:** "A forest trail with layered depth: sharp detailed fern fronds in the immediate foreground, a deer standing in crisp midground focus on the path, and the distant tree canopy dissolving into soft blue-green atmospheric haze."

### 21. Diorama Perspective

**What it is:** Frames a scene as though it exists inside a contained miniature theatrical set piece. Visible boundaries — a box frame, stage edges, or glass walls — establish the scene as an intentional construction, while dramatic spotlight lighting emphasizes the theatrical quality.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "miniature diorama scene," "theatrical box diorama with dramatic lighting," "contained set piece with visible boundaries," or "museum-style diorama with spotlight illumination."

**Example snippet:** "A desert oasis diorama inside a wooden shadow box, dramatic spotlight from above casting sharp shadows from tiny palm trees onto sand-textured base, visible box edges framing the scene, a miniature camel caravan approaching."

### 22. Forced Perspective Scale

**What it is:** Juxtaposes objects of radically different sizes within a shared environment, exploiting perspective tricks to create surreal scale relationships. A figurine appears life-sized next to a real coffee cup, or a full city sits on a desktop.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "forced perspective with dramatic scale contrast," "miniature figure in real-world environment," "surreal scale juxtaposition," or "tiny world placed on everyday surface."

**Example snippet:** "A forced-perspective photograph of a tiny detailed city skyline sitting on the edge of a real wooden desk, office lamp towering over the buildings like a UFO, a real pencil leaning against the tallest skyscraper."

### 23. Containment Scenes

**What it is:** Places a complete miniature world inside a transparent or semi-transparent container object. The enclosing form — a snow globe, crystal ball, lightbulb, water droplet, gemstone — becomes both frame and narrative element, with the interior scene fully detailed at tiny scale.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "miniature world inside a crystal ball," "tiny scene contained within a water droplet," "snow globe containing a complete village," or "detailed world inside a glass bottle."

**Example snippet:** "A complete autumn village scene contained inside a clear glass lightbulb, tiny houses with smoking chimneys, orange-leafed trees lining a winding road, warm interior glow illuminating the glass from within, the lightbulb resting on a wooden table."

## Hybrid Media & Mixed Techniques

### 24. Pencil Sketch + Lifelike Contrast

**What it is:** Juxtaposes raw pencil draftsmanship with fully rendered photographic realism within a single image. Most of the composition remains in graphite sketch form — loose construction lines, shading marks — while one focal element bursts into full color and lifelike detail.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "pencil sketch transitioning to full-color realism," "hand-drawn graphite background with one element in vivid lifelike color," "half-sketch half-photograph composition," or "black and white pencil drawing with a single element rendered in sharp full-color detail."

**Example snippet:** "A pencil sketch of a garden with loose graphite flowers and crosshatched foliage, but the central rose rendered in stunning lifelike detail with full color, visible dew drops, and velvety red petal texture."

### 25. Hand-Drawn Doodles + Real Object

**What it is:** Overlays playful freehand line drawings onto a photograph of a real physical object, creating an interactive relationship between the drawn elements and the photographed subject. The doodles appear to extend, animate, or recontextualize the real object.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "hand-drawn doodles interacting with real photograph," "playful ink sketches on top of product photo," "whiteboard doodle art around a real object," or "cartoon line drawings extending from a real item."

**Example snippet:** "A real photograph of a white coffee mug on a desk, with playful hand-drawn ink doodles emerging from the mug: steam swirling into cartoon clouds, tiny stick figures climbing the handle, and musical notes floating upward."

### 26. Illustration + Photography Integration

**What it is:** Weaves illustrated graphic elements into photographic scenes so that drawn and photographed elements coexist naturally. The illustrated parts may be characters, patterns, environmental effects, or decorative elements that interact with the photographic reality.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "illustrated elements woven into real photograph," "cartoon overlays integrated with photography," "mixed media combining illustration and photo," or "hand-drawn characters placed in photographic environment."

**Example snippet:** "A real photograph of a rainy city street where all the umbrellas have been replaced with illustrated watercolor flowers, each one a different painted bloom with visible brush strokes, casting colored shadows on the wet pavement."

### 27. Action Figure + Real Scale

**What it is:** Places collectible-scale figurines or toy-like miniature characters into real-world environments, photographed at ground level to emphasize the dramatic size difference. The figurine maintains its plastic sheen and joint lines while interacting earnestly with enormous everyday objects.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "action figure in real-world environment," "toy figurine photographed at ground level among real objects," "miniature character in macro photography setting," or "collectible figure scale with real-world props."

**Example snippet:** "A 6-inch action figure of a knight standing at the base of a real potted plant, looking up at the leaves like a vast jungle canopy, morning light casting long shadows across the wooden desk terrain, visible joint articulation on the figure."

### 28. Color Reference Dual-Output

**What it is:** Produces a composition containing two versions of the same subject side by side: a clean black-and-white line drawing suitable for coloring, paired with a smaller fully-colored reference version. This format serves as a coloring guide or style sheet.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "black and white line art with small color reference," "coloring page with color guide thumbnail," "dual output: line drawing and colored version," or "uncolored illustration paired with colored miniature."

**Example snippet:** "A detailed black-and-white line drawing of an ornate butterfly filling the frame, clean outlines with no shading, and a small fully-colored version of the same butterfly in the bottom-right corner showing iridescent blue and green wings."

### 29. 3D Character + Realistic Environment

**What it is:** Drops a stylized three-dimensional cartoon character into a photographically real setting, playing on the contrast between the character's smooth simplified forms and the complex textures and lighting of the real world.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "3D cartoon character in real-world environment with natural lighting," "stylized animated character placed in a photographed real-world setting," "Pixar-style figure in real photograph," or "toon-shaded character composited into realistic scene."

**Example snippet:** "A small round 3D cartoon robot with glossy white shell and glowing blue eyes, standing on a real mossy forest log, natural bark texture with visible lichens and dappled sunlight contrasting with the character's smooth matte surfaces."

## Material & Texture

### 30. Material Transformation

**What it is:** Takes a recognizable object and completely retextures it in an unexpected material, preserving the original form while replacing every surface property. A sneaker becomes hand-blown glass, a car becomes woven silk, a building becomes stacked cookies.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "made entirely of glass," "constructed from woven silk fabric," "rendered in solid marble with visible veining," "sculpted from melting chocolate," or specify the exact replacement material.

**Example snippet:** "A grand piano made entirely of clear blue ice, the keys carved from frozen crystalline slabs, internal hammers and strings visible through the translucent body, condensation beading on the surface, sitting on a frost-covered stage."

### 31. PBR Specification

**What it is:** Controls surface appearance through physically-based rendering parameters that real-time and offline renderers use: roughness determines how blurry reflections appear, metallic values control conductor vs. dielectric behavior, and subsurface scattering simulates light penetrating translucent materials.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "PBR material with high metallic and low roughness," "physically-based rendering with subsurface scattering," "roughness 0.2 metallic 0.9 for brushed steel," or "dielectric material with sharp specular highlights."

**Example snippet:** "A decorative sphere rendered with PBR properties: brushed copper surface with metallic value 0.95, roughness 0.3 creating slightly diffused reflections, subtle green patina in recessed areas with roughness 0.7, under neutral HDRI studio lighting."

### 32. Tactile Texture Emphasis

**What it is:** Foregrounds the physical, touchable quality of surfaces through sensory language that triggers material recognition. Rather than naming the material, this technique describes how it would feel, activating a different kind of visual imagination.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "fluffy and cloud-soft surface," "rough hand-tufted wool texture," "slippery wet porcelain," "crunchy crumbling bark," or any tactile adjective that implies physical sensation.

**Example snippet:** "A cozy reading nook with a cloud-soft chunky knit blanket draped over the armrest, nubby hand-tufted wool rug underfoot, smooth cool ceramic mug on a rough-hewn driftwood side table."

### 33. Surface Finish Control

**What it is:** Specifies the exact finish treatment of a surface, moving beyond base material to describe its condition: brand-new polished chrome vs. decades-old oxidized copper, pristine matte vs. weathered and chipped, factory-fresh vs. battle-scarred.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "weathered patina with green oxidation," "factory-fresh matte finish," "glossy lacquered surface with mirror reflections," "chipped and peeling paint revealing wood beneath," or "pristine brushed satin metal."

**Example snippet:** "An old brass telescope with heavy green-brown patina concentrated in the engravings, polished bright on the grip where hands have worn through decades of oxidation, the lens ring still showing original machining marks."

### 34. Texture Mimicry

**What it is:** Replicates the physical qualities of non-digital artistic media: the ink absorption pattern of lithograph printing, the warp and weft of canvas weave visible under paint, the waxy resistance of crayon, or the smudgy softness of charcoal on rough paper.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "visible canvas weave under oil paint," "lithograph print texture with ink spread," "charcoal on rough drawing paper," "crayon wax texture with paper grain showing through," or "risograph print with halftone dots and ink misregistration."

**Example snippet:** "A portrait rendered in soft vine charcoal on heavy-tooth drawing paper, the rough paper grain breaking up the dark strokes, smudged shadows under the cheekbones, sharp charcoal edge defining the jawline, white paper highlights on the nose."

### 35. Handmade Craft Aesthetic

**What it is:** Infuses images with the warmth and imperfection of human-made craft: visible stitching that wobbles slightly, clay edges with fingerprints, knit texture with occasional tension variations, and the overall sense that real hands assembled the piece.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "handmade with visible imperfections," "hand-stitched felt with uneven edges," "hand-thrown pottery with fingerprint marks," "knitted with slight tension variations," or "DIY craft aesthetic with visible construction."

**Example snippet:** "A set of woodland animal ornaments in hand-stitched felt, each one slightly different in size, visible blanket-stitch edges in contrasting thread, button eyes not perfectly centered, stuffing creating soft uneven roundness."

## Character & Figure

### 36. Character Consistency Preservation

**What it is:** Maintains recognizable identity across multiple image generations by anchoring to specific immutable features: exact hair color and style, facial structure, distinctive accessories, and clothing. This creates a persistent character that can appear in different scenes and poses.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "consistent character with [specific features]," "same character as previous: [list anchoring details]," "maintaining exact facial features, red curly hair, round glasses, freckles across the nose," or reference a character sheet.

**Example snippet:** "The same character from the previous image — shoulder-length black hair with silver streak on the left side, angular jaw, small scar above the right eyebrow, wearing the olive cargo jacket — now sitting in a library reading."

### 37. Selective Stylization

**What it is:** Applies different rendering styles to different parts of the same subject. The face might remain naturalistically detailed while the body dissolves into watercolor, or the figure is cartoon-simple while hands are rendered with anatomical precision.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "detailed naturalistic face with watercolor body," "realistic portrait above the shoulders, abstract geometric below," "detailed hands on a simplified figure," or "face in full render, clothing in flat illustration."

**Example snippet:** "A portrait where the woman's face is rendered with natural skin texture, visible pores, and sharp eyes, but from the collar down her dress dissolves into loose flowing watercolor paint, blue and violet pigment dripping off the bottom edge."

### 38. Dynamic Pose Description

**What it is:** Specifies body position with kinetic language that implies motion and energy rather than static positioning. The pose communicates an action mid-execution, with weight distribution, momentum direction, and muscle tension all suggested through precise physical description.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "mid-stride with trailing leg extended," "leaning forward into the wind," "frozen mid-jump at peak height," "twisting torso with arm reaching back," or any action verb describing the body's movement state.

**Example snippet:** "A dancer frozen mid-leap, back arched, arms swept overhead, toes pointed downward, hair fanning upward from momentum, one leg extended behind at full stretch, captured at the apex of the jump with fabric of the skirt still rising."

### 39. Expression Set Generation

**What it is:** Produces multiple facial expressions of the same character in a single output, typically arranged in a grid. This serves as an emotion reference sheet showing how one consistent face communicates different feelings.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "expression sheet showing six emotions," "character emotion grid: happy, sad, angry, surprised, thoughtful, mischievous," "facial expression reference with consistent character across all panels," or "emotion turnaround sheet."

**Example snippet:** "A 2x3 grid expression sheet of the fox character, consistent design in all six panels: top row shows joyful with closed eyes, curious with tilted head and raised ear, and grumpy with furrowed brow; bottom row shows surprised with wide eyes, sleepy with drooping lids, and determined with clenched jaw."

### 40. Outfit & Accessory Specification

**What it is:** Defines clothing and accessories with the precision of a costume design brief: fabric type, cut, fit, color, layering order, wear condition, and era-specific details. This level of specificity prevents generic default clothing choices.

**How to invoke:** Describe garments with material, color, fit, and condition: "a slightly oversized washed-out denim jacket over a fitted black turtleneck," "scuffed brown leather boots with brass buckles," or "a vintage silk scarf knotted at the throat."

**Example snippet:** "The character wears a fitted navy wool peacoat with tarnished brass buttons, collar turned up, over a cream fisherman-knit sweater with visible cable pattern, dark slim trousers tucked into scuffed chestnut leather lace-up boots."

### 41. Proportion Manipulation

**What it is:** Deliberately distorts the natural ratio of body parts to achieve a specific stylistic effect. Bobblehead style enlarges the cranium to comical size, chibi shortens the body to two head-heights, and fashion illustration elongates limbs to eight-plus head-heights.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "bobblehead proportions with oversized head," "fashion illustration elongated proportions at 9-head height," "chibi body at 2:1 head-to-body ratio," or "exaggerated large hands on a small frame."

**Example snippet:** "A fashion illustration figure with extremely elongated proportions — nine heads tall, impossibly long legs, tiny delicate head, wearing a flowing trench coat that nearly touches the ground, standing in a confident contrapposto pose."

### 42. Pose Matching & Mirroring

**What it is:** Coordinates the physical positioning of multiple subjects so their poses create visual relationships: mirror symmetry, call-and-response gestures, synchronized action, or deliberate contrast between one subject's tension and another's relaxation.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "two figures in mirror-image poses," "synchronized stance facing each other," "contrasting poses: one tense, one relaxed," or "matching hand gestures between the characters."

**Example snippet:** "Two fencers in mirror-image poses, both in full lunge with right arm extended, left arm swept back for balance, facing each other across a narrow strip, their body angles creating a perfect V-shaped negative space between them."

### 43. Anatomical Detail in Simplified Forms

**What it is:** Maintains correct underlying anatomy — proper joint placement, natural weight distribution, believable muscle groups — even when the overall rendering style is highly simplified or abstracted. The skeleton and physics are real; only the rendering is stylized.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "anatomically correct proportions within simplified style," "cartoon style with proper joint articulation," "stylized but structurally accurate figure," or "simplified rendering with correct weight distribution."

**Example snippet:** "A cartoon bear character in a flat illustration style, but standing with correct plantigrade weight distribution on both feet, shoulders naturally wider than hips, arms hanging at proper length, knees slightly bent in a natural relaxed stance."

## Composition & Layout

### 44. Multi-Panel Grid

**What it is:** Divides the output into a structured grid of separate panels — 2x2, 3x3, or asymmetric bento-box arrangements — where each cell contains a distinct but thematically linked image. Character design, color palette, and style remain consistent across all panels.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "2x2 grid layout," "3x3 panel grid with consistent style," "bento box layout with varied panel sizes," or "four-panel composition showing different angles."

**Example snippet:** "A 2x2 grid showing four seasonal versions of the same cottage: top-left in spring cherry blossoms, top-right in summer sunlight, bottom-left in autumn orange leaves, bottom-right buried in winter snow, consistent architectural details across all four."

### 45. Sequential Panel Storytelling

**What it is:** Arranges panels in reading order to tell a brief visual narrative, using the conventions of comics and manga: establishing shot, action, reaction, resolution. Each panel advances the story with escalating emotional or physical intensity.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "four-panel manga sequence," "comic strip storytelling left to right," "sequential narrative panels with escalating action," or "three-act story told in six panels."

**Example snippet:** "A four-panel horizontal manga sequence: Panel 1, a student stares at a difficult exam question; Panel 2, close-up of widening eyes as realization hits; Panel 3, pencil scribbling furiously with speed lines; Panel 4, triumphant fist pump with sparkle effects."

### 46. Text as Compositional Element

**What it is:** Elevates typography from mere overlay into a primary structural element of the composition. Letters become architectural forms, text blocks create visual rhythm, and the relationship between word and image is designed rather than incidental.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "bold typography as primary design element," "large text integrated into the composition," "words forming the structural backbone of the image," or "typographic poster where text and image are inseparable."

**Example snippet:** "The word OCEAN in massive serif letters spanning the full width, each letter filled with a different underwater scene — coral reef in the O, deep trench in the C, sunlit shallows in the E — water spilling over the letter edges."

### 47. Social Media Frame Integration

**What it is:** Incorporates recognizable social media user interface elements — profile frames, like buttons, comment bubbles, story borders — as interactive props that characters acknowledge, touch, and break through, blurring the line between content and platform.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "character interacting with Instagram frame border," "reaching out to tap a floating like button," "posed inside a social media profile picture frame," or "leaning on the edge of a story border."

**Example snippet:** "A chef character leaning out of a circular Instagram profile frame, one arm draped over the border, the other holding a steaming dish that extends past the frame edge, a floating heart reaction drifting up from the plate."

### 48. Trading Card Design

**What it is:** Frames a character or object within the structured format of a collectible trading card: defined border with decorative elements, character artwork in the center, stat bars or text areas at the bottom, and optional holographic or foil effects on the card surface.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "trading card design with stats and border," "holographic collectible card layout," "TCG card with character art and attributes," or "game card format with ornate border and power stats."

**Example snippet:** "A fantasy trading card with ornate gold filigree border, a dragon illustration in the center panel breathing blue fire, holographic rainbow sheen across the surface, stats panel at the bottom reading ATK 8500 DEF 6200, the card slightly tilted to catch light."

### 49. Label & Signage Placement

**What it is:** Places legible text on surfaces within the scene as natural environmental elements: hand-lettered chalkboard menus, neon shop signs, product labels, street signs, or book spines. The text is part of the world, not an overlay.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "chalkboard sign reading [text]," "neon sign spelling [word]," "product label displaying [brand name]," "hand-lettered shop window saying [text]," or specify exact text and surface type.

**Example snippet:** "A cozy bakery storefront with a hand-lettered wooden sign above reading 'Flour & Sun,' a small chalkboard easel by the door listing 'Today: Sourdough, Rye, Cinnamon Rolls,' and a neon OPEN sign glowing warm orange in the window."

### 50. Jewelry & Small Object Design

**What it is:** Renders small-scale decorative objects — enamel pins, pendants, keycaps, earrings — with precise material definition, correct scale, and appropriate display context. Attention to clasp mechanisms, pin backs, mounting hardware, and surface finish sells the object as real and manufacturable.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "enamel pin design with metal border," "pendant on silver chain with gemstone detail," "artisan keycap in translucent resin," or "earring design with specific findings and materials."

**Example snippet:** "A hard enamel pin of a crescent moon cradling a sleeping cat, gold-plated raised metal lines separating midnight blue and soft white enamel fills, two butterfly clutch pin backs visible, displayed on a dark denim jacket lapel."

### 51. Packaging Design

**What it is:** Uses product packaging — boxes, bottles, wrappers, cans, bags — as both the subject and the compositional frame of the image. The packaging design is fully realized with typography, color scheme, material texture, and brand identity as if ready for production.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "product packaging mockup," "artisan box design with label," "bottle label design with full branding," or "retail packaging as hero shot with studio lighting."

**Example snippet:** "A craft chocolate bar package lying on a marble surface, matte black wrapper with embossed gold foil mountain logo, ingredient list in clean sans-serif on the back, the wrapper peeled halfway to reveal textured dark chocolate beneath."

## Narrative & Context

### 52. Story Element Substitution

**What it is:** Creates a visual template built around a narrative archetype or well-known story structure, where cultural or contextual elements can be swapped to produce localized variations. The composition and emotional arc remain fixed; the specific characters, settings, and symbols change.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "visual template based on {story archetype}," "classic hero's journey scene with [cultural setting]," "fairy tale narrative template: swap [element] for local variant," or "universal story structure with customizable cultural details."

**Example snippet:** "A visual retelling of the 'clever trickster outsmarts the powerful' archetype: {trickster character} sitting calmly across a table from {authority figure}, a single overturned cup between them, the trickster's slight smile revealing they have already won."

### 53. Visual Metaphor Construction

**What it is:** Translates abstract concepts into concrete visual symbols arranged to communicate meaning beyond their literal appearance. An hourglass becomes time pressure, a maze becomes decision paralysis, roots becoming cables becomes nature-technology tension.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "visual metaphor: [abstract concept] represented as [concrete object]," "symbolic composition where [object] represents [idea]," or "metaphorical scene illustrating [theme] through [visual element]."

**Example snippet:** "A visual metaphor for burnout: a candle in the shape of a human figure, melted down to a short stub, wax pooled around the base in the shape of scattered papers and coffee cups, the last flicker of flame at the wick barely visible."

### 54. Satirical Commentary

**What it is:** Combines contradictory or incongruous visual elements to make pointed observations about social, technological, or cultural phenomena. The humor or critique emerges from the gap between what is shown and what is expected, using visual irony rather than text.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "satirical juxtaposition of [element A] and [element B]," "ironic scene contrasting [expectation] with [reality]," "visual commentary on [topic] through absurd combination," or "editorial illustration critiquing [subject]."

**Example snippet:** "A satirical scene of a family dinner where every person, including the dog, stares at their own smartphone, while an elaborate home-cooked meal goes cold on the table, a framed 'Home Sweet Home' sign on the wall behind them."

### 55. Historical/Temporal Setting

**What it is:** Applies era-specific visual details — architecture, clothing, technology, lighting, color palettes — to place subjects convincingly in a different time period. Modern subjects gain historical context, or historical subjects receive contemporary treatment.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "set in 1920s Art Deco New York," "Victorian-era London setting with gas lamps," "rendered as if photographed in 1960s Tokyo," or "ancient Roman setting with period-accurate details."

**Example snippet:** "A modern software developer transported to a 1940s newsroom, sitting at a heavy wooden desk with a mechanical typewriter, wearing suspenders and rolled sleeves, but the paper in the typewriter shows lines of Python code."

### 56. Environmental Storytelling

**What it is:** Communicates narrative through background details and environmental context rather than explicit character action. The arrangement of objects, wear patterns on surfaces, and small incidental details tell a story about who lives here, what happened, and what might happen next.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "environmental storytelling through background details," "scene telling a story through scattered objects," "lived-in space with narrative clues," or "setting that implies history through wear and personal items."

**Example snippet:** "An empty park bench at dusk with narrative clues: two coffee cups (one lipstick-marked), a forgotten scarf draped over the armrest, a small wrapped gift left unopened, a single wilting flower on the seat, footprints leading away in two different directions."

### 57. Weather as Narrative

**What it is:** Uses atmospheric conditions as active storytelling or data-communication elements rather than passive backgrounds. Rain intensity communicates sadness or urgency, fog obscures information, lightning illuminates hidden details, and sunshine marks resolution.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "storm clouds reflecting emotional tension," "fog obscuring all but the nearest details," "single shaft of sunlight breaking through as a symbol of hope," or "rain intensity matching the mood of the scene."

**Example snippet:** "A lone figure walking down a rain-soaked street, the storm at its heaviest directly overhead creating a column of downpour around them, but three steps ahead the pavement is dry and a crack of golden light splits the clouds."

### 58. Thematic Declaration

**What it is:** Explicitly states the interpretive meaning or emotional thesis of the image within the prompt, guiding all compositional and stylistic choices toward a unified message. Rather than hoping the AI infers the theme, the creator declares it outright.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "the theme is resilience despite isolation," "this image communicates the joy of small discoveries," "the emotional core is bittersweet nostalgia," or "compositional choices should reinforce the idea of fragile balance."

**Example snippet:** "Theme: the quiet dignity of unglamorous work. A janitor mopping a grand concert hall after the audience has left, a single spotlight still on, sheet music scattered on the stage, the janitor's posture upright and unhurried, reflected in the wet marble floor."

## Product & Object Design

### 59. Commercial Product Photography

**What it is:** Replicates the controlled lighting, precise angles, and flawless presentation of professional studio product photography. Key light, fill light, and rim light create dimension; reflective surfaces show controlled highlights; the hero angle presents the product at its most appealing.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "commercial product photography with studio lighting," "hero shot at three-quarter angle with rim light," "publication-quality product photo on clean background," or "e-commerce product image with soft shadows."

**Example snippet:** "A luxury wristwatch photographed at the classic hero angle, key light from upper left creating a defined shadow, rim light separating the brushed steel case from the dark background, crystal face reflecting a subtle gradient, floating on a clean white surface."

### 60. 3D Product Visualization

**What it is:** Renders products as clean three-dimensional models with controlled material properties, typically in isometric or three-quarter view. Soft uniform shadows, neutral backgrounds, and precise edge definition create the polished look of architectural or industrial visualization.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "3D product render with soft shadows," "isometric product visualization on neutral background," "clean 3D model with accurate material properties," or "product CGI with matte and glossy surface contrast."

**Example snippet:** "An isometric 3D render of wireless earbuds and their charging case, matte white plastic with subtle pearl finish, soft contact shadow on a light gray gradient background, one earbud floating slightly above the open case to show the interior."

### 61. Branded Element Insertion

**What it is:** Incorporates customizable brand identity elements — logos, color schemes, typography, slogans — into image compositions through clearly marked placeholder variables. This creates reusable templates where visual identity can be swapped without redesigning the entire image.

**How to invoke:** Use bracket placeholders like "[brand logo] on the product surface," "[brand color palette] applied to the packaging," "[company name] in the signage," or "[tagline] on the banner."

**Example snippet:** "A coffee cup sleeve design featuring [brand logo] centered on kraft paper texture, [brand color] accent stripe along the top edge, [tagline] in small type near the bottom, cup sitting on a reclaimed wood counter in a bright cafe."

### 62. Object Shape Generation

**What it is:** Creates objects conforming to specific silhouettes or outlines, often derived from emoji, icons, or custom contour specifications. The interior fills with detailed content while the outer boundary maintains a strict recognizable shape.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "shaped like a [specific emoji/icon]," "object conforming to the silhouette of [shape]," "detailed scene contained within [outline] shape," or "custom contour: [description of boundary]."

**Example snippet:** "A succulent garden arrangement shaped precisely like a heart emoji silhouette, various green and purple succulents packed densely within the heart boundary, viewed from directly above on a white background."

### 63. Creative Container Design

**What it is:** Designs functional vessels, planters, holders, or storage objects shaped as characters, animals, or unexpected forms. The container must balance visual personality with practical utility — an opening that holds things, a stable base, appropriate scale.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "planter shaped like [character/animal]," "pencil holder designed as [object]," "functional container in the form of [creature]," or "desk organizer shaped as [theme] with practical openings."

**Example snippet:** "A ceramic planter shaped like a sleeping fox curled in a circle, the hollow of its curled body forming the planting well, matte terracotta glaze with hand-painted white tail tip, small drainage hole underneath, a trailing pothos plant spilling over the fox's back."

### 64. Mechanical Detail Specification

**What it is:** Defines precise engineering and mechanical elements: gear tooth profiles, spring mechanisms, keyboard switch stems, hinge types, and fastener specifications. This level of detail is essential for technically credible illustrations of mechanical objects.

**How to invoke:** Use phrases like "visible gear mechanism with interlocking teeth," "mechanical keyboard keycap with MX-compatible stem," "exposed clockwork with brass gears and jeweled bearings," or "cross-section revealing internal spring mechanism."

**Example snippet:** "An exploded-view diagram of a mechanical wristwatch movement, each component separated and floating in sequence: mainspring barrel, gear train with brass wheels, escapement with ruby pallets, balance wheel with hairspring, all on a dark background with thin connecting lines."

## Parameterization & Reusability

### 65. Bracket Variable Substitution

**What it is:** Uses square brackets to mark mandatory replaceable elements within prompt templates. Each bracketed term represents a slot that must be filled with a specific value before the prompt is usable. This creates structured reusability without ambiguity about what needs customization.

**How to invoke:** Place square brackets around any element that should change between uses: `[subject]`, `[color]`, `[setting]`, `[animal]`, `[time of day]`. Include a legend or inline note listing valid substitution options when helpful.

**Example snippet:** "A [subject] standing in a [setting] during [time of day], wearing a [color] scarf, holding a vintage camera, with [weather condition] creating atmosphere in the background."

### 66. Curly Brace Narrative Insertion

**What it is:** Uses curly braces to mark flexible narrative or cultural content blocks that can be swapped to localize or thematically adapt a prompt template. Unlike bracket variables which replace single nouns, curly brace blocks can contain entire phrases, story fragments, or cultural references.

**How to invoke:** Place curly braces around narrative blocks: `{origin story}`, `{cultural greeting}`, `{local folk tale reference}`, `{seasonal celebration}`. These accept longer, more complex substitutions than bracket variables.

**Example snippet:** "An elder telling a story to children around a fire, the smoke above forming shapes that illustrate {folk tale or legend}, the setting reflecting {cultural architectural style}, traditional {regional textile pattern} on the storyteller's clothing."

### 67. Emoji Substitution System

**What it is:** Designates an emoji placeholder position in the prompt that can be swapped with any emoji to instantly change the theme, shape, or subject of the output. The emoji serves as both a visual shorthand and a semantic instruction, with the rest of the prompt adapting around it.

**How to invoke:** Place an emoji in a position where the prompt structurally adapts to it: "a cake shaped like [emoji]," "the main character is a personified [emoji]," or "pattern made of repeating [emoji] motifs." Document that any emoji substitution is valid.

**Example snippet:** "A cozy miniature room built inside a giant [emoji], the interior furnished with tiny furniture matching the emoji's theme, warm light spilling out through a small window cut into the side. (Replace [emoji] with any emoji: mushroom, pumpkin, seashell, etc.)"

### 68. Audience Targeting Brackets

**What it is:** Includes bracketed demographic or audience parameters that adjust the overall tone, complexity, color vibrancy, and content appropriateness of the output. A children's audience triggers brighter colors and simpler forms; a professional audience triggers refined restraint.

**How to invoke:** Include audience markers like `[age: 3-5]`, `[audience: corporate professionals]`, `[demographic: teenagers]`, `[tone: educational for elementary school]`. These parameters influence style decisions throughout the entire prompt.

**Example snippet:** "An illustration explaining photosynthesis, [audience: elementary school children age 6-8], with a smiling cartoon sun, a friendly tree character with expressive face, bright saturated primary colors, simple rounded shapes, and labeled arrows showing the process."

### 69. Seasonal/Temporal Variables

**What it is:** Embeds time-aware placeholders that adapt the image to a specific season, holiday, time of day, or calendar moment. The variable cascades through the entire composition: season affects lighting, foliage, clothing, and color temperature simultaneously.

**How to invoke:** Include temporal markers like `[season: autumn]`, `[holiday: Lunar New Year]`, `[time: blue hour]`, `[month: October]`. These should influence multiple elements: palette, lighting, clothing, foliage, atmospheric effects.

**Example snippet:** "A neighborhood street scene during [season], with appropriate foliage colors, [season]-weight clothing on pedestrians, [season]-typical sky and lighting conditions, and a shop window display themed for the current [holiday or occasion]."

### 70. Name/Brand Customization

**What it is:** Provides clearly marked positions for inserting personal names, brand names, team names, or custom labels into compositions. These slots are designed to accept variable-length text strings and render them naturally within the scene on signs, jerseys, products, or other surfaces.

**How to invoke:** Mark name positions with labeled brackets: `[person_name]`, `[brand_name]`, `[team_name]`, `[product_name]`. Specify the surface and typographic treatment: "engraved," "embroidered," "printed in serif font," "hand-lettered."

**Example snippet:** "A personalized children's book cover with [child_name] in playful hand-lettered type at the top, the child character (matching [hair_color] and [skin_tone]) riding a friendly dragon over a candy-colored mountain range, 'A [child_name] Adventure' along the bottom spine."
