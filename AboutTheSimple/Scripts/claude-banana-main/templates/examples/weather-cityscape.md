# Weather Cityscape

## Domain Mode
Architectural Illustration

## Technique
Weather as Narrative + Isometric

## Prompt Template

An isometric illustration of {city} rendered in a clean, detailed vector-influenced style, with the city's architecture and street life completely shaped by {weather} conditions at {temperature}. The buildings and landmarks include {landmarks}, drawn with precise geometric edges and consistent isometric projection where no lines converge to a vanishing point. The weather is not merely a backdrop but an active character in the scene — it warps architecture, changes how people behave, alters surfaces, and transforms the color temperature of everything it touches. Puddles reflect neon signs, snow bends tree branches, heat haze distorts distant rooflines, or wind sends debris sailing between buildings. Tiny inhabitants navigate the conditions with appropriate gear, body language, and urgency. The isometric grid allows the viewer to explore the city like a cross-section diorama, with interiors visible through some windows showing people reacting to the weather from inside. The color palette shifts cohesively to match the weather's emotional register — warm and oppressive for heat, blue-gray and contemplative for rain, stark and silent for snow.

## Variables

- `{city}`: The urban setting — e.g., "Tokyo's Shibuya district," "a fictional Mediterranean coastal town," "1920s Chicago"
- `{weather}`: Dominant weather condition — e.g., "a torrential monsoon downpour," "a record-breaking summer heatwave," "the first heavy snowfall of winter," "a golden autumn windstorm"
- `{temperature}`: Specific temperature reinforcing the mood — e.g., "38 degrees Celsius," "minus 12 degrees Fahrenheit," "a cool 15 degrees Celsius"
- `{landmarks}`: Recognizable architectural features — e.g., "the Shibuya crossing, a towering department store, a ramen alley," "whitewashed houses cascading down a hillside, a blue-domed church, fishing boats in the harbor"

## Aspect Ratio
1:1

## Usage Notes
This template merges weather storytelling with isometric city views to create rich, explorable scenes. Nano Banana Pro (gemini-3.1-flash-image-preview) handles both architectural precision and atmospheric effects with strong results. The key to a strong result is choosing extreme or visually dramatic weather that forces visible changes in the environment — mild conditions produce less compelling images.

## Example Output

An isometric illustration of Tokyo's Shibuya district rendered in a clean, detailed vector-influenced style, with the city's architecture and street life completely shaped by a torrential monsoon downpour at 28 degrees Celsius. The buildings and landmarks include the iconic Shibuya crossing with its zebra stripes dissolving under sheets of rain, a towering department store with illuminated signage reflected in the flooded pavement, and a narrow ramen alley with steam billowing from kitchen vents into the wet air, all drawn with precise geometric edges and consistent isometric projection where no lines converge to a vanishing point. The monsoon is not merely a backdrop but an active character — rain hammers against awnings creating curtains of spray, gutters overflow with rushing water channeling between buildings, umbrella canopies form a dense colorful mosaic above the crossing, and every glass surface streams with rivulets that fragment the neon glow into abstract color. Puddles reflect the red and blue signs in wobbly neon mirrors on the asphalt. Tiny inhabitants navigate the deluge with transparent umbrellas, rolled-up trousers, and hurried postures, while a delivery cyclist splashes through an intersection. The isometric grid allows the viewer to explore the district like a cross-section diorama, with interiors visible through rain-streaked windows showing a couple sharing ramen, a shopkeeper watching the downpour from a doorway, and an office worker pressing a palm against foggy glass. The color palette shifts cohesively to deep slate blue, electric neon pink, warm ramen-shop amber, and the silver-white of falling rain, matching the monsoon's humid, energetic emotional register.
