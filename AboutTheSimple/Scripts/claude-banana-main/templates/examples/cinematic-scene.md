# Cinematic Scene

## Domain Mode
Cinema

## Technique
Dutch angle composition with chiaroscuro lighting

## Prompt Template

A tense cinematic frame of {character} caught in the act of {action}, set within {setting}. The camera is tilted at a subtle Dutch angle, roughly ten degrees off-axis, introducing visual unease without becoming cartoonish. A single hard key light rakes across the scene from camera left, carving deep shadows that obscure half of the character's face in a chiaroscuro pattern. The emotional register reads as {emotion}, conveyed through body language, micro-expression, and the tension between the character and the surrounding negative space. Practical set dressing — peeling paint, scattered objects, ambient dust motes caught in the light beam — builds narrative texture. The depth of field is razor-thin, isolating the subject against a bokeh-softened background rendered in muted complementary tones. Shot on Kodak Vision3 500T stock with a vintage Cooke Speed Panchro lens, lending gentle halation around highlights and a warm, organic falloff in the shadows.

## Variables

- `{character}`: The person in the scene — e.g., "a rain-soaked detective in a rumpled trench coat," "a young cellist with ink-stained fingers," "an elderly lighthouse keeper"
- `{action}`: What the character is doing — e.g., "reading a crumpled letter," "holstering a revolver," "turning toward an unseen sound"
- `{setting}`: The environment — e.g., "a dimly lit 1940s phone booth," "a crumbling Baroque chapel," "a neon-drenched back alley in Taipei"
- `{emotion}`: The dominant feeling — e.g., "quiet dread," "reluctant resolve," "suppressed grief," "defiant calm"

## Aspect Ratio
2.39:1

## Usage Notes
Ideal for narrative-driven images, character concept art, or storyboard-style compositions. The Dutch angle technique should be used sparingly — it loses power when overused. This template shines with Nano Banana Pro (gemini-3.1-flash-image-preview), which responds well to cinematographic language and film stock references.
